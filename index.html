A.I Will Empower Humanity

By Reid Hoffman, originally published in Jan. 25, 2025
Annotations by Griffin Cooper.

Assignment: 18 Pitfalls

Reid Hoffman is both a co-founder of LinkedIn and a board member at Microsoft. He currently has a "priority to invest and build with AI to benefit humanity", at least according to his LinkedIn page. The focus of this annotated article will be covering how the author of this opinion piece has fallen into some of Kapoor and Narayanan's 18 pitfalls of AI journalism.
I will be annotating for as many of these logical errors as I can find but as this is an inherently biased article written by a significant investor in AI, I just can't reasonably cover every pitfall a single statement can make. For the sake of being concise and a total of <1500 words, I'll be only using one comment per sentence.

I recently learned of a new way people are using artificial intelligence. “Based on everything you know about me,” they ask ChatGPT, “draw a picture of what you think my current life looks like.”

Like any capable carnival mind reader, ChatGPT appears to mix safe bets with more specific details. It often produces images of people sitting in a home office with a computer. Perhaps an acoustic guitar sits in the corner or an orange cat prowls in the background. But also on occasion something like, say, a large head of broccoli will be sitting in the middle of the desk.

Pitfall 3: Comparison with Human Intelligence | Pitfall 16: Downplaying Human Labor
Though a common theme, the author compares the output of AI to the capability of a human performing a job. Realistically, we all know that an AI is capable at what it's been trained to do, but it entirely neglects the social aspects of a "capable carnival mind reader". The showmanship of a carnival worker is the majority of the experience, especially when compared to the output of AI generated imagery.
It's worth noting that it isn't just that this is allegorical for "doing tricks" as a carnival worker would: the immediate comparison is to a human, and the automatic assumption is that the chatbot is doing something a human could do.

Off-kilter elements like that are what give these portraits not just their quirky charm but also flashes of epiphany. By absorbing the wide-ranging mix of work questions, personal goals and everything else that makes up our ChatGPT history, the system teases out patterns and connections that may not be readily apparent. In this way, these portraits don’t just reflect. They also reveal. Presented with such depictions, a user may be compelled to ask: Am I really mentioning cruciferous vegetables in my chats so often that ChatGPT thinks they’re a central part of my life?

Pitfall 4/5/10
The claim is that through "absorbing" every piece of information you've ever inputted into AI, it can produce "patterns and connections that are not readily apparent". This is not how AI works. It quite literally takes all of the given information, puts it on a very, very complex map, and spits out the output based on that. When it comes to images, it does this by taking the most common areas of visual clarity with general color matches. It is taking the most probable route to whatever conclusion it predicts you most likely want: By its very nature, it will not reveal anything that is not already readily apparent.

As a board member at Microsoft and an early funder of ChatGPT’s developer, OpenAI, I have a significant personal stake in the future of artificial intelligence. But my stake is more than just financial. I truly believe that by giving billions of people access to A.I. tools they can use in whatever ways they choose, we can create a world where A.I. augments and amplifies human creativity and labor instead of simply replacing it.

Pitfall 7/12/17/18
Simply put, AI is not meant to accomplish that.
The vast majority of use cases for AI are present in the accessibility or cost-effectiveness of an AI over a human. It's ludicrous to think that billions of users would use AI on a daily basis, even if AI companies somehow had the capacity for these users and billions of globally applicable use cases to go along with it. Moreso, to say that a tool designed to do something a person could do, but for less expense, would somehow "amplify human labor". Each drawing generated is one that did not come from our own hands, each essay written one that did not hone skill or present struggle. If those opportunities we pass by for momentary convenience do not help build creativity, then it's hard for me to say what does.

That’s why I find these ChatGPT portraits so fascinating: They clarify and dramatize enduring concerns about identity and privacy in the digital age. How much exactly is ChatGPT remembering? They implicitly ask. How judiciously is it processing these memories, and who benefits most when it does? As a user of these technologies, do you sense that you’re being monitored in ways that make you feel as if you’re being exposed, controlled and manipulated? Or do you feel seen?

Pitfall 1/12
AI doesn't think. It takes information, matches it to its massive database, and predicts what the most common responses are to that information matching its many different tags at different weights. It's fun to believe that the AI can think and decide things, then change based on that like a person but it does not actually do that: It's a distinction the author knows as a tech guru, but does not make for the sake of their argument.

Few truly powerful technologies come without any risks. Perhaps third parties with different motives and values from your own will somehow gain access to the data. Once made aware of your past patterns, these third parties might be able to effectively anticipate and influence your future decisions. While I recognize that some people see such risks as disqualifying, what I’ve found through my own experiences is that sharing more information in more contexts can also improve people’s lives.

Pitfall 14/15
Just because something is unlikely to happen does not mean it cannot happen. The author dismisses all of your personal info getting stolen as just a risk, and then immediately states that "sharing more information in more contexts can improve people's lives". As the author is directly overseeing some of the world's largest AI projects under Microsoft, this is an extremely concerning statement and stance to take toward user privacy in AI. Nonetheless, this also just doesn't address the limits on what an AI does and does not store: It certainly doesn't save every single sentence ever sent, but it may save certain weights or parameters that correspond to a user or their general inputs based on a pattern.

In our concern about potential harms, it can be easy to overlook the many positive effects technology has had. I co-founded LinkedIn, a professional social network, more than two decades ago, but I still get a steady flow of missives from people who have found jobs, started businesses or made promising career changes because of interactions they’ve had on the platform. And this is all because they’re willing to share information about their work experiences and skills in ways that were once considered both imprudent and impractical.

Pitfall 5/10/12
LinkedIn is not responsible for the creation of resumes. While it is a convenient platform for putting a CV out for recruiters, reaching out to potential employers or recruiters using a resume or CV is a practice that long precedes the platform.
Recruiters do reach out to candidates on the site now, which is a more recent occurence due to the platform. However, it is not because they are sharing information in a new fashion: It is because that information is conveniently consolidated in one place. It's a function of social media platforms in general over a function of how sharing work experience has changed via media.

Tech skeptics have long used the adjective “Orwellian” to cast everything from a video recommendation feature to turn-by-turn navigation apps as threats to individual autonomy, but the history of technological innovation in the 21st century tells a different story. In “1984,” George Orwell’s classic novel of state oppression, powerful telescreens enable a totalitarian regime to rule over dispossessed proles with unchecked omnipotence. But today we live in a world where individual identity is the coin of the realm — where plumbers and presidents alike aspire to be social media influencers and cultural power flows increasingly to self-made operators, including the one-man podcasting empire Joe Rogan, the YouTube megastar MrBeast and the human rights activist Malala Yousafzai.

Pitfall 5/8
While arguable, this isn't actually true. Having an identity is marketable, but you can just as easily not create and market fame, and instead become rich through alternative means. This also ascribes individual identity as the identity of an individual as percieved by the masses instead of one's actual individual identity.

I believe A.I. is on a path not just to continue this trend of individual empowerment but also to dramatically enhance it.

Imagine A.I. models that are trained on comprehensive collections of your own digital activities and behaviors. This kind of A.I. could possess total recall of your Venmo transactions and Instagram likes and Google Calendar appointments. The more you choose to share, the more this A.I. would be able to identify patterns in your life and surface insights that you may find useful.

Decades from now, as you try to remember exactly what sequence of events and life circumstances made you finally decide to go all-in on Bitcoin, your A.I. could develop an informed hypothesis based on a detailed record of your status updates, invites, DMs, and other potentially enduring ephemera that we’re often barely aware of as we create them, much less days, months or years after the fact.

When you’re trying to decide if it’s time to move to a new city, your A.I. will help you understand how your feelings about home have evolved through thousands of small moments — everything from frustrated tweets about your commute to subtle shifts in how often you’ve started clicking on job listings 100 miles away from your current residence.

Pitfall 3/7 This is what humans do to reason: We analyze, we make decisions based on the person that we have become through the shaping of millions of tiny events adding to a persona far larger than the sum of these individual actions. AI does not do this: It predicts, based on millions of similar events, what the most likely outcome based on those factors is.
This also has the implication that an AI can and even will have access to everything you do online, presented as a net positive. Personally, I would not want everything I do analyzed at every given moment pushed through an AI owned by a private corporation.
The author speaks largely of how he wants AI to "amplify human creativity". All I can see when he speaks of this is the slow degragation of human creativity as we lose the ability to develop the skills for creativity, replaced by the allure of convenience and the slow death of effort.
As people, we need to struggle a little. We need to be wrong, to work out what things are to us instead of simply having every available answer immediately given to us. If the first option at every corner is AI, I don't think people would excise the self-control needed to spend some time, work something out, and make their own conclusions.

For those who choose to pursue this new reality, the tools that make it possible are multiplying and evolving rapidly. Developers of all sizes have been introducing apps and features that enable you to automatically record, store and analyze virtually anything — or everything — you do on your PC, phone and other devices. In doing so, they turn such data into the material for a de facto second self, one that can endow even the most scatterbrained among us with a capacity for revisiting the past with a level of detail even the novelist Marcel Proust might envy.

Pitfall 4, 10 Having detailed information about a subject does not make the stored data a 'second self', it just gives you the ability to interpret data on that subject with more accuracy. AI can do that, previous comments about how it 'thinks' in mind: But trying to interpret a person's behavior and psyche based on how they use the internet over how they interact with each individual thing in their lives is inaccurate and somewhat short sighted.
People generally use the internet as a tool, to accomplish some kind of purpose each time it is used: We do not know why they intend to accomplish that goal, nor why they are doing it in the way that they are.

There’s more to this shift. While critics of Big Tech often emphasize how A.I. can empower corporations to use people’s data for manipulation or discrimination, we can also deliberately design A.I. to give individuals greater facility to derive insights from their own data. What if you had an AI that could analyze your browsing patterns and alert you when advertising algorithms were successfully manipulating your purchasing decisions? Or one that could detect when social media algorithms were steering your attention toward increasingly extreme content?

Pitfall 15
Advertisers have had a massive cultural and legal victory over the internet: It is commonplace, even expected for an ad to appear in any available spot in any non-professional website. Critics of big tech say that AI can use people's data for manipulation/discrimination because it inherently will: If advertising was dissuaded from using their bought data to target advertisements, a market gap for targeted advertisements would open up. Not even mentioning the consistent 13th pitfall overarching the entire article, this data would be used by for-profit companies. To clarify, advertising data that was still gathered but not used to advertise products to consumers is potential profit left off the table by an AI company that would tell users they were being manipulated by adverts: If an AI could tell you that you were being manipulated by an advert, it would mean that it would be able to generate those adverts to manipulate you. And it is the natural inclination of a for-profit company to increase its dividends by any means possible, so that data would be put to good use by such a company.

To some degree, we all self-track and always have. We make to-do lists and keep journals of our daily activities. We weigh ourselves and record our daily steps or the number of miles we jog, generally in pursuit of some kind of self-improvement or at least self-awareness. Ultimately, ongoing cycles of reflection, action, assessment and refinement are how humanity progresses and expands what it even means to be human.

So imagine a world in which an A.I. knows your stress levels tend to drop more after playing World of Warcraft than after a walk in nature. Imagine a world in which an A.I. can analyze your reading patterns and alert you that you’re about to buy a book where there’s only a 10 percent chance you’ll get past Page 6.

Pitfall 5/16
We do not do things based on whether we may or may not fail. We try to keep doing them because we want to, because it'd be enjoyable, because it'd change us a little. The idea that knowing you may not commit to something isn't how we would amplify human creativity: We do it by thousands of these little failures, until we can succeed.

Instead of functioning as a means of top-down compliance and control, A.I. can help us understand ourselves, act on our preferences and realize our aspirations. In this way, perfect recall isn’t just a tool for remembering the past. It’s also a compass that provides a clearer understanding of our goals and improves our decision-making. It transforms our digital trails from passive records of who we were into dynamic resources, empowering us to shape who we wish to become — with greater self-awareness and freedom to live lives of our own choosing.

Pitfall 5/12/13
Everything the author has claimed his idea of a future AI can do does not align with their mission to "augment and amplify human creativity, instead of replacing it." Every single use case presented by the author is a replacement of what a person could do, what we actually do, or what we want to do. To amplify something is to boost it beyond what it currently is, and what they have shown it doing is taking away the potential for the base value of human creativity and innovation that AI is meant to expand upon. We would not be finding freedom. We would be losing our ability to see it.

Instead of functioning as a means of top-down compliance and control, A.I. can help us understand ourselves, act on our preferences and realize our aspirations. In this way, perfect recall isn’t just a tool for remembering the past. It’s also a compass that provides a clearer understanding of our goals and improves our decision-making. It transforms our digital trails from passive records of who we were into dynamic resources, empowering us to shape who we wish to become — with greater self-awareness and freedom to live lives of our own choosing.

Throughout the article, the author presented the 13th pitfall throughout nearly every given example of their future idea of AI. They consistently show how they view their perspective of AI over the reality of it in a plethora of pitfalls, and that they view their idea's maximum potential of AI as an extension of it should it exhibit linear growth over time. AI is already slowly plateauing: It cannot continue at its current pace, nor will it maintain a signifigantly larger population (See DeepSeek's various errors on launch due to user density.) Should AI still manage to maintain its growth, it will still not accomplish the author's stated goal: To amplify human creativity and labor, instead of replacing it. Because it was not a machine built to amplify what a human can do: It was a thing that has always been designed to do it in their place.
